Charlotte from the Netherlands

 



Charlotte's story

Charlotte was born on the borders of the Waddenzee (lowlands), where her grandparents had a farm and her father owned greenhouses. Every week she used to take long walks on the beach with her father. From these memories, and her father's character, she developed a deep love of the Wadden. Charlotte's father was the first organic farmer in the Netherlands. As a child, she had to deal regularly with questions relating to the environment, healthy food, and the natural world.

In order to go to secondary school, Charlotte had to leave the countryside where she grew up. She joined a band of hippies at the age of seventeen, and stayed at weekends with friends in a squat, which was like a commune. It was on an old farm in the country, and there the young student revolutionaries philosophised about the world:

In the early sixties there was a big difference between the city and the countryside. I was an exception at school. In 1966, a movement arose that expressed itself in long hair, smoking hashish and so on. Because I was an exception in that fashionable school anyway, I joined that movement.

After secondary school, Charlotte wanted to travel. Her parents disagreed, but she was determined to see the world on her own. She hitchhiked to London and Paris, where she wrote music and poems. Later on she started to study at a school in Utrecht. She never finished her study, because she ended up in Morocco with a friend. At that time, she was broke and had to beg for money. After three months of roaming, singing, and begging Charlotte finally came back to her home town. She started to live in a commune again. Later, at the age of twenty-one, she decided to go back to her parents in order to get her degree at the Agricultural School. She finally graduated in geography, and went back to live in the commune again.

Once back in Groningen, she finds out that her beloved Waddenzee is in danger and she immediately joins a movement to preserve this fragile nature reserve. She is very successful in her activities and becomes a paid coordinator. Because of her direct and vigorous approach, she gets a lot done, but her direct approach eventually also leads to a conflict with her superiors, and she was dismissed from her duties. Charlotte explains:

I did make wrong political assessments sometimes, but you learn from that. You try to handle issues just firmly, but then you notice that the issue is very sensitive and that you should have handled it more strategically. In fact I was acting like a bull in a china shop. On the other hand I have also achieved a lot.

Her oldest son is autistic. In order for him to have the best life possible, Charlotte fights for his rights in an organization for parents with mentally handicapped children. She is convinced that both the Waddenzee and her son would not have been in the more healthy position they are in today, if it was not for her.

In order to get anywhere, you have to have guts, work hard, be passionate, and you should be able to brag now and then.

(Written by Joël van Raak and Ruud van der Veen)
