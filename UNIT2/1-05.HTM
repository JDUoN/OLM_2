Ursa from Slovenia

 



Ursa's story

Ursa's parents were intellectuals and artists. They had a big influence on her early aspirations to become an artist. When she was ten years old her parents were divorced, which had a big impact on her, as she explains:

Something good came out of it. At that time I was only ten years old and very passive, but after their divorce I became very active and I wanted to observe the people around me.

As a high school pupil, Ursa tried to initiate changes in the grammar-school system, and got into conflict with one of the teachers when she disagreed with a proposed solution, which she thought was unjust. She dropped out, and moved to London without her parents' consent. In London she took a job in a travel agency, where she tried to improve the employees' working conditions, but without success. She subsequently left the firm.

Seven years later she moved back to Slovenia. Having worked in various capacities, raising a child as a single mother, learning about social work and NGO initiatives, she returned to Slovenia as someone with much experience in life. Settling back in Slovenia was a difficult time in her life. Although she took various jobs, her income did not cover all her costs. A gesture of solidarity came from an Italian friend, who collected money from his friends to cover her bills, which strengthened Ursa's conviction that solidarity among people is essential.

I have to say that at that time this resolved my financial situation and brought me a whole range of new ideas: that some people like to help others without necessarily actually knowing the person; and that solidarity among people exists, in spite of the fact that at that time we were very oriented towards the consumer society.

Ursa started to work professionally as a social worker in a Children's Hospital. She began to realise that families were excluded from the treatment of their children. She therefore sought new models of family treatment. Despite the rigid institutional and professional barriers, she succeeded in introducing innovative ideas; her methods helped parents to be with their children while they were in hospital. In Slovenia before the transition to democracy, changes in any sphere of life, even in the private domain, were very difficult.

At this time Ursa received a British Council grant to visit Children's hospitals in Oxford and London in England. This opportunity gave her the chance to explore Family Therapy techniques and, as a result, she organised the first family seminar in Slovenia. She also joined the new initiative Crisis Line where she worked as a supervisor and team member. As a team member and researcher, she participated actively in national projects and in international conferences. Her papers focused on children, youth and family issues. She was also a cofounder of the Association for the Prevention and she created a network of youth mentors.

Through her work in different public services, Ursa promoted voluntary work as having special value for health and social care. Cooperation with many students and volunteers made her realise that civil initiatives need to be supported to be able to be transformed into civil actions. Therefore she became the president of the Association for Preventive and Voluntary Work with the main goal of supporting civil initiatives and promoting voluntary work.

The war in the Balkans took Ursa into the battlefront, helping in the refugee crisis.

When refugees fled, first from Croatia and then from Bosnia, Ursa decided on a new direction:

When watching this war, I spent half a day lying on the couch and staring at the television, and then all of a sudden I got up and stamped my foot on the floor with such force that I surprised myself; and I said to myself, that this was enough, that I had to do something, that I can't be passive any more.

In a matter of days, Ursa managed to gather a hundred volunteers when the first refugees arrived. Her strong belief, that each individual has the knowledge and skills needed to support civil initiatives, guided her new activity. Projects in the camps included cultural activities, sports activities, psycho-social support, legal assistance, youth work, and so forth. Active participation of the refugees became one of the most important aims. Ursa learned that besides giving personal assistance to refugees, lobbying and advocacy activities on the national and international level were very important. In the period 1991-1995 she was also very active in peace initiatives in the former Yugoslavia.

(Written by Angelca Ivancic, Darka Podmenik, Darijan Novak)
