Jyrki from Finland

 



Jyrki's story

Jyrki is a part-time politician and a computer enthusiast whose biography shows a person in search of new possibilities through new information and communication technologies.

Jyrki considers himself to be a person with multiple identities:

… If you go to my personal homepages, you will find a list: a human, a scientist, an author and a politician.

He explains why he had to produce four parallel homepages because of the multiplicity of his identity:

Yes, actually it was unavoidable, ... . And this became obvious when I started to build my homepages. Similarly, I noticed that it was going to become an uncontrollable mishmash, it was not going to work at all, (I am speaking) about the structure of my homepages. So, I actually had to produce three homepages for me: one for a scientist, one for an author and one for a politician….

As a human being Jyrki says he fell in love while he was at school! He was engaged to his childhood sweetheart in 1980 when both of them entered to the upper secondary school. He then married his fiancée in 1989. The first child was born in 1994.

As a scientist Jyrki works at the Helsinki University of Technology. His studies have been connected with electronic performance support systems in learning organizations.

As an author Jyrki has written dozens of articles and books related to computers and the Internet. He has also written a couple of science fiction short stories and a radio programme series. In addition, he had spent a couple of years as the editor-in-chief of Kosmoskynä, the journal of the Finnish Science Fiction Writers' Association.

Jyrki became a politician in late 1994. Rather than complaining about things, he wanted to offer solutions to problems. Moreover he wanted to offer an alternative solution to problems. This alternative was realised as a new party, the Young Finns Party (Nuorsuomalainen puolue - There are historical reasons for the name and it was not aimed just for young people). He took part in collecting 5000 signatures to establish a new political party, and took part in the 1995 parliamentary election. He used the Internet to carry out his election campaign. He claims that:

... I am the first Internet politician in Finland. Except for the latest municipal elections, I have organised all election campaigns almost totally on the Net, because I hadn't a so called election budget i.e. I had to use the tools that I had, and especially the tools I had for free.

This first Internet election campaign brought 403 votes, which were helping a party colleague of his to enter the Finnish parliament. In the 1996 double election his 1185 votes were not enough to get him to the EU-parliament, but he succeeded in the city council election and became a councillor and a member of the city board too. In the 1999 parliamentary elections no candidates of the Young Finns Party were elected to the parliament and its members decided to dismantle the party. After that Jyrki found a new political home in the Green League. In the year 2000 municipal elections he got re-elected to the city council from the Green League list.

It looks also that Internet campaigning is not enough on a level of parliamentary elections. It is enough on a level of council election, but not yet on a level of parliamentary election. Of course, much will happen on the Internet during the next three years. But unfortunately, from the viewpoint of voters it gives you credibility, when you sometimes have advertisements of 10 000 Finnish marks in newspapers. People somehow subconsciously feel that you are serious. From somewhere I should find that 20 000 Finnish marks. There have been, even in council election, 300 000 Finnish marks campaigns. It always comes to mind a question over who has paid that 300 000 and why.

Besides carrying out election campaigns on the Internet, Jyrki has regularly updated his home pages on the Internet. In his role of a politician he has described his working as a councillor and a member of the city board. These pages include, of course, some elements of self-congratulation but also serious discussions with existing and potential voters about values, opinions and achievements such as initiatives and decisions that he had made.

(Written by Matti Laitinen & Kari E. Nurmi)
