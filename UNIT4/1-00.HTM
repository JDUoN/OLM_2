Overview

How do people learn Active Citizenship and Governance?

Active citizenship implies individual and/or collective acts of social responsibility, towards others or on behalf of others. Active citizenship often also involves governance, i.e., participation and involvement in decision making processes that affect wider society. Our main question in this theme is how people learn to take this kind of social responsibility. In your work you may have come across many active citizens, contributing in various ways to their communities and their families. How do they become active citizens? What are the factors that contribute to that transformation? You may have a lot of experience about this from your own life and work.

We will explore these issues through the stories of two people we've met, Beryl from England and Jeffrey from Belgium. Through Beryl's story, we'll identify the main factors that contributed to her learning. We will also examine the main features of the learning process. Through Jeffrey's story, we'll identify three main conditions that help the learning process: challenge, capacity, and connections. Based on these two stories, and the other stories that you have read so far in other Themes, and your own reflections as you read the stories, we'll attempt to identify some commonalties in relation to learning active citizenship. Finally we ask the question: how can we facilitate the learning of active citizenship? This will be the main focus of Theme 5.

Before reading these stories, let's pause for a moment ...

Reflection

Think of your own life story.

  • How did you become an active citizen?
  • What kinds of things do do you as an active citizen? How did you learn to do them?
  • Are there any factors that you can identify that contributed to your becoming an active citizen? Can you describe your own learning process?
