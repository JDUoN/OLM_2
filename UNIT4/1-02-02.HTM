Learning process - Challenge, Capacity, and Connections

From Jeffrey's life, we can identify three conditions which play a significant role in people's learning process, to be active citizens. We call these challenge, capacity and connection. Let's look at each of these in detail.

Challenge exists in specific settings, but can constantly change. It can be a challenge or threat to oneself, which encourages feelings of solidarity with others who are in a similar situation. Or it may be a challenge faced by others, which gives one a sense of being needed. There are two types of motivation: person-oriented and society-oriented (though a combination of both types is often involved). Often a theme important in one's personal life is also valuable for others, and becomes a focus of broader social or political action. Today many people try to balance their personal and societal challenges. They are active citizens, but their engagement has to fit their personal meanings and aspirations.

Capacity refers to the feeling of being able to influence one's surroundings. It involves the skills, knowledge, capacities, strategies and experiences needed effectively to make something happen. It is a sense of agency, a feeling of being able to make a difference, a feeling of being capable of having an effect on a situation. Recognition, respect and support from others strengthen this.

Connection is about real connections with other people, ideas, movements and organisations. It is important to be constantly open to other perspectives, ideas and people.

Challenge, connection and capacity do not have to be inaccessibly vague terms. In Jeffrey's story, it is seen that there are many individual starting-points to stimulate these three conditions in order to learn active citizenship. You do not have to be highly educated or have many specific skills. In fact, everybody can recognise important experiences that can stimulate and improve one's activities as an active citizen.

(Written by Carmen Mathijssen & Joël van Raak)
