Beryl's story

2: Personal circumstances and critical incidents

At 19 Beryl was a single mother of a young child. Later on she had two more children. Without many educational qualifications, she struggled to find suitable employment. She studied and later became a nurse, while taking care of two young children while studying. This was a transitional period in her life. Later on she was involved in work concerning the welfare of single mothers and young children. This involvement is related to her own life and learning experience during this period. She says that taking care of young mothers and babies was:

... something close to my heart because I was a single young mother with children and I was in a working environment with single mothers and their children …

Working in such an environment was also helpful for her to develop her awareness of the issues facing disadvantaged people. The particular organisation she worked with also had religious affiliations, which point towards her religious influence (pointed out in the previous page) as well.

She also mentioned about 'not getting on well with [her] relationships'. While she did not go into details, it appears that this is also a transitional period. During this period, she attended classes in women and society at her local college. In these classes she interacted with other women coming from similar socio, economic and cultural backgrounds, which helped her to develop her awareness of society (more of this in the next page).

Beryl's experienced a number of critical incidents: a life-threatening illness, unemployment, and her son's serious accident. Just after her 40th birthday, she was diagnosed as having cancer, and just as she was recovering, her son met with a serious accident. She was unemployed at that time. She had to take care of her son who was on a life-support machine. She explains what she learned during this period:

I saw women as carers and how they could take care of their children from maybe birth, and they would be still taking care of some children into adulthood depending on the kind of illness that they had.

She learned that that the carers and mothers do not get as much support as they should get. Consequently she developed a self-help group because she felt that 'we probably could help ourselves better.' This critical incident, and her 'life time experiences' with 'disadvantaged women, lone mothers, carers' who 'find it difficult to access certain benefits and things' motivated her to found a self-help organisation for women. For her work, she was recently given a national award for women who make a major but unrecognised contributions to society.

She also mentioned 'a bad patch in [her] life.' She was unemployed for 9 years, following her illness. After she recovered from her own illness she found it difficult to get back to employment, which again triggered her active citizenship:

I found it difficult to get back to employment, … in the sense that my self-esteem had lowered and I didn't know how that was going to be picked up again, and I think that happened to a lot of people who become unemployed. Once you have lost your job sometimes it is difficult so you have to go back into a retraining mode and for me there wasn't a middle thing to build you up to get back into the job. [People] need another [intermediate] step in order to get back so I think that women in particular needed this little extra …

She founded an organisation that provided the 'little extra', a 'drop in centre', a safety net for vulnerable women. She explains the functions of the centre:

If you are unemployed, if you are looking after young children, you can come and be with other women, have some discussions, self help, learn a skill, you might be able to move on to something else …

At work too Beryl came across a number of transitional periods and critical incidents. For example, in the early period of her life in England (soon after completing the nurse training) she could not continue to work as a nurse in the hospital; she became a community nurse. Although she says that working as a community nurse 'brought [her] closer to people' the main reason for this transfer was that, at the hospital 'the hours were long and the money was not good to bring up a family'. She was a single mother of three young children at that time. This new work was useful for her later work as an active citizen. As a community nurse she had to 'use [her] own initiative' and 'prioritise [her] workload.' She learned to be more independent and resourceful.
