Learning process - a transformation

Developing the sense of agency

We have identified three inter-related factors as contributory to Beryl's becoming an active citizen: values and attitudes formed since early socialisation, personal circumstances and critical incidents, and the development of a critical awareness of society. Over the course of her life, these factors helped her to develop her sense of agency along three dimensions: identity, responsibility, and capacity.

The three inter-related factors contributed to the development and changes in her identity. The identity is both personal (who she is) and social (groups that she identifies with and the connections she make). She has been taking on responsibilities towards the causes that were suited to both her personal and social identity. Beryl had taken responsibilities towards her own family, and towards the groups that she identified with such as Black ethnic minorities, single mothers, and various disadvantaged groups. Through her activities she developed her capacity, learning many skills.

Learning- a transitional process

The learning process, as the story illustrates, is very much an informal, incidental, and ad hoc process where a number of interacting factors and critical incidents played a main role. At times she was not aware that she was learning. At times, she was struggling to cope with her life - so the learning sometimes was an outcome of how she managed to cope with the critical incidents in her life.

Based on Beryl's story, and many others, isn't it fair to say that, especially in the case of adults, learning occurs in response to experiences of tension? And as a result the individuals take steps towards acts of governance and active citizenship? In Beryl's case, attitudes and values formed from early socialisation had already prepared a fertile ground for her becoming a 'good' and perhaps an active citizen. Her family and religion were significant influences. Her life however is marked by an array of personal circumstances and critical incidents which transformed her life - an immigrant with little formal education, a young single mother of three surrounded by poverty, at the age of 45 suffering from a life-threatening illness, unemployment while recovering from the illness, having to look after her son on a life-support machine - all these personal circumstances transformed her into an active citizen taking part not only in the welfare of her own family, but also the governance of local communities.

Her early socialisation and the values that had already been fostered were important in enabling her to handle the critical incidents and transitional moments in her life. In addition to learning from these experiences (informal learning), Beryl also took part in a number of formal and non-formal courses at the local college and other institutions. Enrolling in nurse training was her first formal learning, after moving to England at the age of 19. While she was a nurse, she studied at night classes in her local college in order to 'build [her] education up' and for 'personal development.' She studied 'the simple things - maths, English, and social skills.' Later on she studied about women in society which helped her to understand more about women's issues. While she was looking after her son in hospital, she took an introductory course to learn information technology skills. These skills were useful later when she founded the women's voluntary group. Various learning experiences also contributed to her development of a critical awareness of society, mostly with regard to the issues relating to those personal circumstances. She both contributed to change existing organisational structures and created new ones in order to address the issues which she was interested in.

Reflection
