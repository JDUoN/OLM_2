Learning to become an active citizen - some key points - activity checkerboard.

The attributes necessary to become active citizens are learned incidentally, in socio-institutional and cultural processes.

Learning active citizenship is not a linear, a one-dimensional, or a neat process. The process is often accidental, unexpected and ad hoc. Informal modes of learning seem most important.

Not all informal learning of citizenship is equally valued. We need to re-examine the particular skills, which can play a crucial role in developing active citizenship among traditionally excluded groups.

People learn to become active citizens due to many reasons (e.g. strong socialisation in the family, critical or frustrating experience, a critical awareness of the societal conditions, etc.).

Three important conditions that facilitate the learning and the practice of active citizenship are Challenge, Capacity, and Connections.

Learning active citizenship can happen in all areas of life: private, work, civil society, and state.

Learning in the private domain (home, family, friends) is seldom recognised as an important location for learning about, and the practice of, citizenship.

Women active citizens have a tendency to be connected to care and family responsibilities; these roles are central to their active citizenship.

Women's role within families and societal expectation about motherhood influence their sense of agency and career development. For many women, career breaks for family reasons are a feature.

Younger women perceive themselves as having better chances for equality in work and family relationships than older women.

Higher education, both formal and nonformal, has helped some women to resist stereotypes and develop their own individual identities.

Education can help women to find new skills and to take new and active citizenship roles.


If the learning process is incidental, and informal, is there anything that educators, and facilitators do to strengthen the learning of active citizenship. We'll examine this in Theme 5, the last theme of this learning material.
