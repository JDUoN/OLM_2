Encouraging and Supporting 'Catalysts' for Change

This category of programmes encourage individuals to become 'catalysts' for change in their communities.



Can-doers

One particular umbrella programme is the Can-doers programme supported by the Scarman Trust , a national charity formed by Lord Scarman after his report on riots in the inner cities in the 1980s. The Scarman Trust is 'dedicated to helping people gain greater power over their lives, especially by formulating new "deals" between community-based organisations and decision makers in government.' The underlying philosophy of the programme is to empower people so 'they are not alienated from power and institutions'. The Can-doers programme enables local communities to be involved in governance, and help them to make a difference in their own communities. The individuals identified as catalysts are resourceful people, already 'at work, setting up saving schemes or food clubs, getting young people off the street and into sports or the arts, renovating estates, or setting up not for profit businesses to achieve their aims.' They need some support, such as finances and connection to other networks (e.g., the local government, civil service, and other local community workers). The Scarman Trust helps with resources to about 500 can-doers countrywide.

Community Champions

This is also within the Scarman Trust, a scheme run in collaboration with DfES to increase community activities. There are about 150 people Community Champions in London. These programmes enable the individuals to build confidence in their capabilities and have a 'snowball effect' which will lead to more effective projects. The enterprising individuals and organisations who make efforts to make a change in their communities will form new 'civic networks [that are] the 21st Century equivalent to the parish pump.'

Outreach and Networking

Surrey Police also runs a programme to encourage such catalysts, with the intention that they will eventually become individuals doing outreach work with disaffected sections of the community. In most cases, one person working with certain communities develops a network, and subsequently becomes a catalyst for change in the community. A number of outreach programmes have been initiated around sexuality and ethnic minority issues. These initiatives have helped the individuals to discover their hidden skills and learn new skills. The individuals who were involved in these programmes have experienced 'enormous personal growth after volunteering', and learning has been 'interesting' for them.
