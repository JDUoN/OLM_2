Approaches with a 'Training' Component

Page 1 (of 2 pages)

Some programmes contain a significant portion of 'training' in various aspects of citizenship, for example, European citizenship, leadership skills, etc. Following are some examples.

Training for Life Skills

One example is a programme that operates in an area of social deprivation. The programme encourages people to take responsibility for their own communities, e.g., through taking courses in gardening, decorating, and basic skills of literacy and numeracy. These provisions have enabled people to build confidence and to move away from drug abuse and crime. Courses that improve life skills have helped people to get back to work, and to volunteer in community activities.

Learning Citizenship in Europe

CiCERO is an example of a project aimed at adults to develop their understanding of, and their participation in the European dimension of citizenship. This is a one-year programme for 'people who are unemployed, at home with children, retired: people who are "doing something locally" - as tenants, with play groups, as helpers in schools, youth clubs, in schemes for local job creation … .' The objective of the programme is to enable 'local people to develop the skills and confidence to help end the divisions in our society: helping to tackle poverty, bad housing, poor schooling, racism, disability, and discrimination …' and for those who want to get back to education and employment. Initial participants from ex-mining communities went to Brussels to investigate exclusion and citizenship in the EU context. The programme is now organised as modules, which explore citizenship, democracy and the EU, and social exclusion. A study visit to Brussels is included. At the time of investigation, there were 13 groups with 130 people involved, from across the country and different walks of life.

The programme is in five sections, three run locally, and two held on a residential basis at the Northern College of Adult Education. During the year the participants go to Brussels to meet community groups and decision-makers in the European Union. The three local sections are held one morning a week for ten weeks: 30 weeks in all. The residential parts at Northern College are over three days (two nights) and the study visit to Brussels is five days (four nights). The discussions are around points such as: who makes decisions and how people can make an impact ('democracy, citizenship and Europe'), why some people get a worse deal than others ('social exclusion'), how the European Union works and what it can do for the local community ('understanding the EU'), and how one can access funds ('understanding Funding').

The participants share experiences with others and meet similar groups from different parts of the UK and mainland Europe. Modules include: Europe, Democracy and Citizenship, How Europe works, Social Exclusion and Employment, Study-Visit to Brussels, Fundraising. At the end of the course, participants are expected to be more confident, have an understanding of the social, political and funding processes which affect their community, and be able to use what they have learnt. They also get nationally recognised credits. The programme is free, funded by the National Lotteries, the European Union and the DfES. The programme also provides childcare facilities to the participants.

Learning Democratic Processes

There are other programmes that focus on imparting skills to enable people to take part in community activities and the democratic process. The targeted participants are members of excluded communities or groups. An example is a computer-training programme given for 250 survivors of domestic violence to enable them to interact with MPs on-line and give their views to a Parliamentary Select Committee. Some of these had influenced subsequent legislation. A strong community developed and continued as a support group. These were the initiatives of the Hansard Society as part of their political education programmes. Another programme, under the Can doers of Scarman Trust, offers English language skills to the immigrant communities, enabling them to build their skills and confidence to be involved in the community, once they arrive in this country.
