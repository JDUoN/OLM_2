Facilitating Interaction among Communities and Agencies

We have come across a number of programmes that aim to encourage and strengthen active citizenship through providing opportunities for interaction among different social groups and agencies. The underlying assumption is that people and communities learn best through interaction, learning from each other. Following is a number of such projects.

'Bringing People Together'

Some of these programmes cater to communities in deprived areas and housing estates. In some cases these programmes also contain an element of loosely formalised curriculum. An example is a weaving project, run by the Workers' Educational Association (WEA) on a deprived housing estate in Sheffield. The curriculum helps people to engage in artistic and creative skills and explores social history and gender roles. In addition, the programme enables the exchange of ideas between different social groups, e.g., between white working class and a Somali refugee group. 'Bringing people together' in this way can have a 'positive effect' on people who are marginalised and isolated.

Exploring European citizenship

This is a programme run by the WEA in South Yorkshire which is aimed at community activists, to explore and learn about European citizenship. The programme, now operating nationwide including Scotland and Northern Ireland, brings people together through learning by experience, networking and partnership with other agencies. The project also has an emphasis on family learning, e.g., parents and carers engaging with their children's learning and thus their own learning, taking on School governorships, opening up schools for broader use, etc. According to the participant who reported this programme, the benefits of the programme to the individuals and the communities has been: 'moving from alienation to involvement', 'building relationships and trust', and 'increasing levels of self-esteem.'

Church for Un-Churched People

This is a programme organised by the Baptist Church in Cranleigh, Surrey. About 300 people meet regularly in a school, with the participation of the local authorities and the school. Participation within small groups, using the Bible as a source material, and linked to community projects, is expected to foster learning active citizenship and governance.

Reducing crime through community policing

A similar community-based volunteer programme, with the help of the local authorities, works towards reducing crime through community policing. This programme uses both formal and informal settings, and a variety of teaching methods such as workshops, role-plays, case study analysis, discussions and feedback sessions to explore crime and community safety issues.
