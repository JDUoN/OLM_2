Community Learning (England)

The learning programme illustrated in this story is a non-formal education programme conducted in an area of South East England. It addresses a range of issues concerning social problems arising from today's developments in civil society.



Learning in a deprived area

The area became socially deprived following the closures of a large defence establishment in the early 1980s. The area comprises both large modern estates (where people have been 'thrown together') and areas of older housing with varying social problems.

Loss of livelihood became a main social problem, and resulted in loss of confidence among working-class groups, according to adult educators who work there. This loss of confidence had become a main contributor to many social problems, particularly in some of the housing estates:

those who were stripped of their livelihood lost confidence in themselves and many people had been through drugs and petty crime but now wanted to improve. They thus needed the confidence … .

As one educator pointed out, the loss of jobs and the disappearance of traditional working culture has affected people's civic engagement.

Their formal civic engagement tends to be diminishing. Men are completely alienated. Alienated to the extent that getting up in the morning [is impossible] … . They are sitting at home, [in front of] their televisions … . Their self esteem has been damaged ... .

The adult educators stressed the importance of building confidence as a key element of educational interventions/facilitations. The educational programmes being carried out in this area

aim to empower people to take responsibility for their own communities

These educational activities use a variety of participatory activities such as gardening and decorating. Some people benefit from learning basic skills in computing to keep up with their children, while others benefit from learning basic literacy and numeracy skills. Courses in life skills help people to learn new skills which could be transferable for volunteering and community involvement, such as taking responsibilities on committees. The educators feel that

some people do want qualifications as a means of getting back into work.

A variety of programmes of learning, even extending to masters level, have therefore been developed and building up networks between different communities.

A main task for the adult educators in areas such as this has been engaging working class men in both learning and in community involvement.

Educators stressed that educational programmes for those who have lost their livelihood and consequently their confidence must be geared towards 'developing confidence and self-esteem':

[to] get these guys away from the television ... , first thing is somehow we have to give something which encourages and values their [involvement] in projects, and get them to see that they themselves can get out of this cycle.

The educational approaches initiated in this deprived area have had some success in increasing people's self-worth and subsequently their civic engagement.
