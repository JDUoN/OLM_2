Study Circles (Slovenia)

The Slovenian Institute for Adult Education (SIAE) introduced study circles in 1992. They are a very popular type of non-formal adult education in Slovenia. In eight years they have reached over 10,000 people all over the country. Study circles enable quality time to be spent studying, and enhance social capital. They also encourage lifelong learning, reduce functional illiteracy and help develop civil society.

The basic principles of study circles are equality, democracy, cooperation between participants, active participation and action, freedom to set objectives and plan, and freeing resources. These principles make study circles appealing to participants, who make decisions about their own learning depending on their own interests and problems. The method gives equal importance to learning and social activity. Learners explain how they take part in study circles:

First we only discussed things and got to know each other, then we set our objectives, and then we prepared our plan how to realise these objectives. We acquired all the literature needed, then the fieldwork started and the final objective was the exhibition. Later we added some new elements and now it has become a kind of meeting place, where people meet after Mass, they sit and talk. I think that it has long-term benefits because it boosts the community's self-esteem.

Mentors find appropriate locations for the meetings, and invite participants. They usually invite people from their communities although they also advertise in the local media. Later organisational work is divided among the participants. They jointly choose the time, place and pace of their learning. They also take a personal approach.

You do not start with professional terminology and expertise, but with the person. … You have to pull out of anonymity people who usually do not have that opportunity.

The variety of subject-matter dealt with in study circles is large. Participants can learn about medical herbs, mushrooms, legal rights of senior workers, project work, organising international scientific conferences, managing NGO's, the Slovenian war of independence, opportunities for the blind in towns, various handcrafts, and so forth. Readings and discussions give deeper insights into many themes.

After learning about these topics the participants transfer the knowledge to their environment. So-called 'action goals' are defined in the form of publications (books, postcards, regular newsletters), printed and taped materials (brochures, articles in regional and national papers, guides, Websites), presentations and public events (exhibitions, workshops, radio and TV contributions) and other events (celebrations, food tasting, concerts, dramas, excursions, literary evenings, etc.)

Besides satisfying participants' personal and social needs, study circles are also important for their communities. This is especially true in underdeveloped regions of Slovenia where other forms of education and social activity are more or less absent. The group activities and learning which take place in these locations have a very positive impact on people's everyday lives.

Mentors and participants help the local communities to develop, driven by their enthusiasm and strong will to learn, teach and act. Study circles offer a different culture of learning, and one which is closer to home.

(Written by Darijan Novak)
