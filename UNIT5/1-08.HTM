Community Regeneration and Capacity Building

These programmes place the emphasis on empowering excluded communities though regeneration activities and capacity building, both individually and as a community.



Capacity Unlimited

Capacity Unlimited is a company created in 1994 to develop structures and manage European funds to support voluntary services within the local communities. The service delivery focuses on advice, guidance, vocational training and capacity building and has targeted members of communities excluded from many mainstream services and programmes. Within this programme, refugee groups and ethnic minorities give advice on issues affecting local communities. According to the programme organisers, this programme helps people learn to be confident, 'a key empowerment indicator', which fosters active citizenship. The organisation works in partnership with a variety of agencies and institutions in the UK and across Europe. Services range from project management to consultancy, evaluation and research and project bidding and design. Over the years the organisation has developed expertise in project management methodologies and models concerning the delivery of community regeneration projects, which is an integral part of the organisations work.

Issues facing Refugee Groups

This category of programmes places particular emphasis on issues facing refugee groups, and their integration into the society. An example is a programme concerning the Moroccan community, enabling the children of the community to fit in with the wider society. They have set up a youth football team and mentoring with help from the Scarman Trust. The programme uses entertainment to encourage the young people to understand and explore their identities. Providing opportunities to meet other similar minority groups in Europe is a key feature here. For example, the police have given funding for a trip to France to meet other Moroccan young people there.

Adult and Community Learning Fund

Adult and Community Learning Fund, an initiative within the Basic Skills Agency also has initiated a number of learning programmes aimed to build capacity of individuals and communities. An example is a programme to improve basic skills as an important condition for active citizenship. An example is a local neighbourhood project, aiming to improve the residents' capacity as individuals and as an organisation. According to the project organisers,

members of tenants groups often have poor basic skills and without the right support they may never feel able to participate in their community.

In this project the group members developed their basic skills so that:

they could take part in in-house committee meetings, be able to work with other tenants effectively and be able to attend council meetings and feel able to communicate with the council in both verbal and written form. As a consequence they began to develop a vision of their role in the democratic process.

Other work within ACLF includes work with a local Citizens Advice Bureau. Here the clients with poor basic skills have been given opportunities to improve their basic skills especially linked to their needs such as debts, and given guidance on routes of progression. The outcomes of the project are significant:

These [participants] may have never become learners without this project; many felt learning was not for them and felt unable to access college based courses.'.

Another project is run with the Big Issue (an initiative to help the homeless) focused on supporting Big Issue salespeople to 'develop their learning and hopefully move on.' The programme was called Learn to Earn; Learn to Live and Learn to Work. The module Learn to Live particularly focused on citizenship and health issues. Building on the experience gained from this project, ACLF has developed a pack called Citizen 2000 which focuses on Consumer, family, neighbourhood and law issues.
