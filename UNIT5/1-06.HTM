Learning through Volunteering

Page 1 (of 2 pages)

Volunteering can help people to learn as well as to practise active citizenship. There are many organisations providing opportunities for volunteering for young and old. Community Service Volunteers (CSV) in the UK is one such non-government organisation that has been working since the mid 60s with young people. CSV believes that young people learn effectively, often 'most effectively', when they engage with 'real life situations, particularly in meeting actual needs and challenges' of communities they live in. Following are a number of CSV programmes that aim to train young people to become active citizens.



Involvement in real-life projects

This programme involves schools, colleges and universities in the UK, drawing about 45,000 young people a year. These young people learn and develop citizenship skills through tackling real life projects that enrich the local communities and the environment. Through peer learning, they learn 'team building, positive values, planned action, reflection, evaluation and celebration of achievement.' Learning in this kind of environment promotes 'key skills for life, work and citizenship,' 'positive attitudes and values', and a 'habit of lifelong learning.'

Millennium Volunteers

This is also a national scheme that encourages 16-24 year olds to build on their individual interests through volunteering. Over 4,000 young people are involved in the UK and projects range from mentoring to DJ-ing. Volunteers can give up to 200 hours of their time and receive a nationally recognised certificate when they finish. Some participants carry on volunteering with local youth groups after being volunteers when they were at college or school.

Volunteering opportunities for children with learning difficulties

This scheme aims to provide volunteering opportunities for children with learning difficulties, instead of putting them at the receiving end of support. Pupils with severe learning difficulties at Portland School and Felstead School have become Millennium Volunteers through CSV, working alongside other young volunteers on an educational conservation project. The effect on pupils according to the Tertiary Coordinator at the school is positive:

Volunteering is the only area where our pupils are on an equal footing. They can gain a qualification like everyone else and at the same level. It is a huge learning curve.

There are 14 Millennium projects run by CSV, contributing to a UK-wide network of 95 projects run by different partner organisations.

Student tutoring

Entitled CSV Learning Together, this is a nationwide programme launched in 1992. Currently there are about 160 universities and colleges with active student tutoring schemes. Each year more than 7,000 students volunteer and over 1,000 schools benefit from having student tutors in their classrooms. Under this scheme, university and college students volunteer to work alongside teachers in local schools, helping pupils with their studies, raising their aspirations, and encouraging them to go on to further and higher education. This programme operates through a network of regional advisors and a national office providing advice, networking opportunities, conferences, workshops and a range of resources. Student tutors spend one morning or an afternoon a week, for an agreed number of weeks, helping in the classroom with subjects across the whole curriculum. Through student tutoring students can develop their communication, organisation, problem solving and social skills and get involved in their community.
