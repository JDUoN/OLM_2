Participatory Learning (Spain)

In the late 1970s people in the neighbourhood of La Verneda - Sant Martí took to the streets demanding a civic centre (for local cultural activities) and a school for adults. They wanted a particular public building to be converted to such a centre. Their demonstrations included conducting classes on the street in front of the doors of the building. After many actions, the local people won their demand; the civic centre and a school for adults was established, which is now called La Verneda - Sant Martí School for Adults. This school and the civic centre offers a number of principled approaches to facilitating learning and active citizenship.

La Verneda's approach to learning is based on a number of core principles that suit the adults and the community they live in. At La Verneda, learning occurs in and with the community, and people are able to take an active role in their learning, leading to improvements in the community. The transformation that can be seen in the neighbourhood today is just one example of the outcomes of such an approach.

Offering equal opportunities for everyone to participate is another main aim of the school. A main emphasis has been placed on encouraging participation by disadvantaged people. La Verneda - Sant Martí School does not charge fees to its learners, which makes it possible for everyone to attend classes, without having to think about the cost. In addition, the school also opens from 9.30 a.m. until 9.45 p.m. These long hours permit a wide range of schedules. The result is that almost everyone can find a suitable time to attend classes and learn.

La Verneda's also uses new methods and new technologies. La Verneda believes that the new technologies can be tools for social inclusion, and for facilitating active citizenship, by making such technologies available for its learners. The Ágora´s OMNIA point in La Verneda - Sant Martí School for Adults is an example. The OMNIA point is a space organised with computers and open to local people at no cost. At each OMNIA point, introductory courses are provided for those who have never had the opportunity to develop computing skills.

People's skills are the starting point, according to La Verneda's philosophy. It is important to begin with what people can do, not with what they can't. Everyone has skills; everyone has developed their particular skills according to the context of their daily lives. Active citizenship asks for people to be treated as having and developing a lot of qualities that can be used in a variety of situations. It is important to work with people's present potential and to emphasise that they can make a difference. People should be given the message that active citizenship does not always require specific skills, but can be achieved in a variety of ways, starting from one's own capabilities. At La Verneda - Sant Martí, for example, the knowledge and skills a person has gained from prior experiences is used as a starting point for learning new knowledge and skills.

Coordination of different activities in different domains is another priority. Active citizenship can be achieved in different areas, but to make it more effective the different domains and levels of action (local, regional and state) should be coordinated. Coordination should involve fully understood and freely made commitments to participate. La Verneda - Sant Martí School for Adults, participates in various activities organised and coordinated by VERN, the Coordinator of organisations in the neighbourhood. They are also involved in European and regional level projects which provide resources for all who study at the school. In addition, there are networks established between various training centres for adults across Europe.

La Verneda also provides an open space for democratic participation. La Verneda believes that active citizenship cannot be stimulated if the organisation itself does not function democratically. Democratic organisations can provide space for decision-making and participation. People learn more while participating than by being taught to participate. People develop several skills and abilities when participating in various settings. In La Verneda school, for example, there is a monthly meeting open to the adults who attend the school, as well as teachers and volunteers. At the meeting they provide information about the activities of the school, and themes and issues are debated by the whole group before decisions are reached.

Breaking power relationships is also La Verneda's priority. The traditional power relationship between the teacher and student is one of expert and participant. The teacher has knowledge (power), while the student is passive, inferior, and does not 'know' enough to be able to contribute anything. It is essential to break this power relationship. Everyone can contribute. At La Verneda, the participation of adults is encouraged in every class, and opportunities, such as interactive groups, are created which favour team work and autonomy.

La Verneda emphasises dialogical learning based on egalitarian dialogue. Dialogical learning emphasises solidarity, and takes place in classrooms which are democratic, and provide equal possibilities for decision-making and participation for all. At La Verneda - Sant Martí School for Adults, the literary gatherings provide good examples of dialogical learning. These are weekly meetings where people read a classic work of literature and discuss it. The discussion which follows is open and egalitarian: everyone can express their opinions. Dialogical learning through open discussion takes place within a democratic classroom, where the opportunity to participate and make decisions is equally open to all.

(Written by the Team members from CREA, Barcelona)
