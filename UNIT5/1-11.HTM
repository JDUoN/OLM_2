Facilitating Active Citizenship - Some considerations

In Theme 5 we explored the issue of How can we, as educators and facilitators, support and facilitate learning for active citizenship and governance. We have looked at a number of educational approaches and intervention strategies available for active citizenship and governance in six European countries, including England. Through the stories we attempted to identify their main features and their achievements. We wanted to understand how these approaches strengthen the process of becoming an active citizen.

Many of the approaches that we have examined are not new. They have been subject to the test of time. Based on the stories, we have identified ten key considerations that are likely to be useful in developing intervention strategies for encouraging active citizenship and governance. Based on your own experience and your reflections on the stories, you may have more to add. We invite you to add to this table, and compare your views with your colleagues'.

1

Providing an integrated programme of activities, instead of education/training alone

Education and training can impart some aspects of knowledge and skills with regard to active citizenship and governance, but people also need structures to apply what they learn, and need supporting mechanisms on the ground. These would include access to other support mechanisms, funds, and other networks that are operating on the ground. Programmes such as those initiated by the Scarman Trust and Capacity Unlimited are some examples of this approach. The Scarman Trust works with individuals and helps them to be connected to networks consisting of local government, civil service, local community workers etc., which are vital for the effectiveness of an individual's effort. The approach taken by the Capacity Unlimited approach is also towards helping individuals with a package of help, rather than education/training alone.

2

Encouraging greater participation in local communities and promoting the building of social networks

This includes local community and employees. How we lead our lives at present and the new work patterns means there seems to be a trend towards decreasing traditional networks and support mechanisms. Coupled with this is the diminishing state sponsorship of public services. Educational programmes that are geared towards building networks and greater participation of the local communities, such as those encouraged by the CSV would be effective and valuable in the future.

3

Helping people get back to employment and freeing them from social exclusion

There is some indication that paid employment, at least to some, is an important condition for constructing their identities which has a positive impact on the development of their sense of agency. Examples are available where unemployment has a negative impact on the community involvement. Therefore future effective programme should include some degree of opportunities for people to get back to employment. And such programmes would help people to improve their self-esteem and to be empowered.

4

Integrating learning for active citizenship with work

People (those who are employed) live most of their lives at work, therefore, the workplace needs to be considered as an important place where people learn. People learn a variety of transferable skills at work, which they can use as active citizens. They also bring into work the skills that they learned from being active citizens in their communities. Education interventions fostering active citizenship that are integrated with their work would be effective and beneficial to individuals.

5

Training teachers and other facilitators in building their own capacity and skills

As the debate on education for active citizenship and governance comes to the fore of the agenda in governments and European policy, it is important that there are learning opportunities for educators (both school teachers and community educators) to improve their own knowledge and skills to teach citizenship and cultivate active citizens in communities.

6

Encouraging young people to take part in citizenship and governance

Young people are future adults, and if we are to develop a population of active citizens we need to think ahead and educate the future adults, as portrayed by the many programmes developed by CSV and Civilia foundation. With the introduction of new curriculum in citizenship in schools in England and Wales, innovative approaches (e.g., MAKS in Belgium, 'Citizenship Schools' in the UK (see Alexander, 2001)) are being suggested as ways encouraging schools to develop democratic practices in the school community. Such approaches would 'make citizenship an everyday experience for young people.' Educators believe that 'interventions at a young age work better', and that learning active citizenship needs to be continued from primary school age onwards, because 'for young primary school children, active citizenship comes naturally, …, then when [they] get to the secondary school age, they "switch off", they become uninterested. Therefore it would be important to have some kind of continuation with these pupils from primary school onwards.'

7

Addressing issues related to schools and children's education

Many of the civil society organisations have initiatives that aim to help the schools and children combat the problems they face. Helping young people to achieve would help them to be future active citizens. Therefore, programmes aiming to combat exclusion, bullying, low achievement, low attendance, etc. would be effective programmes in the future.

8

Cultivating a greater understanding of the multi-faceted nature of the society

The European society is increasingly becoming heterogeneous in many ways: race, ethnicity, religion, economic conditions, etc. Recently we have witnessed frictions among communities, such as those in Bradford in the UK. An important element of education for active citizenship would be to educate people to be members of a tolerant society.

9

Addressing the issues of citizenship within the European context

The trend towards greater European integration is increasing. People can make informed choices when they know what these issues are and how to deal with them. Future effective education interventions need to include the European dimensions of citizenship, as exemplified by the programmes such as CiCERO and WEA projects.

10

Exploring the potential of the Internet in the delivery of educational interventions for active citizenship and governance

A few newly developed, partly Internet-based educational interventions are becoming available, such as some programmes offered by the Institute for Citizenship, Women Learning Online offered by the Trade Unions, and Getting Involved and Influential being planned by the National Youth Agency. Internet-based learning is becoming popular in other areas of education, and it is worthwhile to explore the potentials and the barriers that this medium offers for educational interventions on active citizenship and governance.
