Overview

As we have seen in previous themes, particularly in Theme 4, learning active citizenship in adults is a lifelong process. People seem to learn what they need to become active citizens incidentally, in social and cultural processes. These processes are informal. A variety of reasons and factors, such as strong socialisation in the family, critical or frustrating experience, a critical awareness of the societal conditions, etc., can contribute to these processes. Challenge, Capacity, and Connections are important for people to become active citizens. Your own reflections may have generated more ideas.

How can we, as educators and facilitators, support and facilitate learning for active citizenship and governance? Can we make any interventions in this regard, to an informal process of learning? This is the big question that we are going to tackle in Theme 5. We will look at a number of educational approaches and intervention strategies available for active citizenship and governance in six European countries, including England. Through the stories we will attempt to identify their main features and their achievements. We want to understand how these approaches can strengthen the process of becoming an active citizen.

In order to facilitate this, we'll first look at examples of approaches from the six countries. We have collected this information through discussions with educators such as yourself from each of the six countries. In each country more than 30 educators and people involved in communities contributed to this process. The story from England tells us how a community in a deprived area learned to fight marginalisation, while Slovenia and Finland provides example of the use of study circles in their countries. The Netherlands provides an example of networking . The Spanish story is about participatory learning in communities. Belgium provides a story of an attempt to create a democratic culture in schools, to prepare children as future active citizens.

After these stories, you'll read some particular approaches in community development and for learning active citizenship. Learning through volunteering, interaction among communities and agencies, community regeneration activities and capacity building, and encouraging and supporting "catalysts for change" are the approaches illustrated in these stories. We also illustrate some approaches with a significant portion of a 'Training' component. All these stories come from England.

Finally, based on these stories, we will identify a number of key considerations that are very likely to be useful in developing intervention strategies for active citizenship and governance. Based on your own experience and your reflections on the stories, we invite you to add to this list.
