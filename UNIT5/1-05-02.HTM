Learning Citizenship at School (Belgium)

School can play an important role in stimulating and strengthening the qualities and competencies of pupils towards active citizenship. But to do this effectively the school itself should provide essential conditions within the system. Amongst others teachers have to see pupils as competent co-actors and take them seriously instead of treating them as 'kids' who are not ready to participate. MAKS, part of the magazine Klasse (for teachers, parents and pupils) attempts to create these democratic conditions in schools and encourage children's participation and communication in school.



MAKS is a democratic initiative, catering for 14 to 19-year olds. MAKS informs pupils about their rights, about the offers that are available for them, to make them more involved with their education and society at large. Also MAKS provides a social platform to stimulate tendencies in society which lead to participation. They want to stimulate youngsters to participate by giving them the feeling that they have everything in them they need to be an actor. MAKS presents positive images: they give examples of boys and girls who are positively involved. They show that youngsters are people with capacities. MAKS stimulates people by showing that it is possible to realise social change.

MAKS wants to 'sell citizenship, as being sexy and fun'. People need to be challenged and informed in a contemporary and attractive way. Moreover the challenge should be easily accessible and not threatening. It must be concrete and related to the life world of individuals and only asking for a precisely defined investment. It is necessary to keep the challenge 'alive' for example by campaigns and repeated and varied messages.

Practices of active citizenship need an 'experimental space' to come about and to grow, which MAKS is attempting to provide. This is a space where free initiative links up with their own experiences, and capacities. There is a structure, but inside that structure there is space for experiments. People, individually and collectively, need to have the space to participate in their own different ways and have to be given the competencies to do so. What is at stake is the creation of a social support base and the necessary conditions for several forms of participation. It is important to give people opportunities to spontaneously take on challenges, develop the feeling to make a difference and feel connected. People learn more while participating than when formally learning to participate. People develop several skills, competencies… when participating in various settings. They not only have to learn to participate, but especially learn while participating.

A concrete example of an experimental space at school, created by MAKS is "change your desk". Youngsters can change for one day with some other student. For example: a student in Latin and Mathematics can change with somebody who studies to become a baker. MAKS creates the conditions to make such a thing possible. The project 'change your desk' is about giving pupils the opportunity and stimulating them to follow another subject at school or in another school. The purpose is to prick their standard view, to awake their interest in other pupils and subjects, to strengthen their feeling of connection. Another example is an international exchange between schools. Stories and testimonies of other people with different experiences in different living conditions can provoke recognition, solidarity and reflection. Exchange projects can confront people with other realities and urge them to look for possible solutions together.

Far too often, participation in experimental spaces runs the risk of being limited to a 'playground', being strictly separated from the real public debate and real policy decision making. In some schools for example, the councils for pupils seem to be created only for the sake of appearance. They do not give pupils real opportunities to take part in the school policy, but are limited to an insignificant parallel circuit. Another risk in this situation is 'participation compulsion': an obligatory participation structure following predetermined rules and conditions, while overseeing the ongoing participation of individuals and their needs, aspirations… Traditionally often the challenge provoking active citizenship (and the way to meet it) is defined beforehand by those who are planning and carrying out the intervention. What's more, it happens on behalf of the participants, who have almost no opportunity to co-define the challenge or to bring forward what interests and motivates them.

School is considered to be a place where citizens are modeled and moulded. The experience of children however is different; most of them experience school as a place where every spontaneous initiative is suppressed. MAKS aim to change this by focusing on promoting skills necessary to participate in society.

(Written by Carmen Mathijssen)
