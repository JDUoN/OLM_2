Learning through Volunteering

page 2 (of 2 pages)

Peer learning

This is a somewhat similar programme, but carried out through peers of their own age range, with the objective of providing 'a structure to the informal learning that happens in the playground, on the school bus, and at the youth club.' These young people's peer education projects encourage mutual help among the youngsters. They would

feel confident that they are in control of their own lives, knowing that they are equipped to deal with peer pressure, make informed choices and decisions and feel good about those choices, and take responsibilities without fear' for their identities and actions.

The scheme was first run in 35 schools. There is a book published to promote this approach (The PeerAid book, CSV/Ibis Trust Publication).

Training peer tutors

One example is the The Pennywell Peer Tutoring Project, which is being carried out in an area that suffers from high unemployment and low morale. The project aims to tackle the effect this has on pupils and their families, through a cadre of peer tutors. The project encourages pupils to learn from their own experiences by addressing problems in the school and sharing this learning with their peers. Since the project began more than 100 peer tutors have been trained to offer support and understanding to 1,100 pupils at Pennywell School. In partnership with the school, CSV is producing resources to help other schools set up similar projects. The programme has a positive impact on the community, according to one teacher:

People here say the one thing that is lacking is self-esteem. If pupils suffer from this, the need to involve parents becomes paramount, yet some parents can feel even worse. They get to a certain stage and think. "That's it, my life is over. I've got nothing to offer, just kids to look after." This work helps pupils and parents to build their self-esteem and confidence.

Volunteers/mentors from the local community helping school children

Under these schemes, parents, local employees, and the wider community are involved in schools. Both pupils and volunteers benefit from adults' support, in both general classroom help and specific mentoring situations. Also involvement by adult volunteers can 'dramatically increase communication between adults and children in the classroom.' CSV has published a book entitled, Absolutely No Limits, to promote this approach. Another scheme that involves the community for the benefit of the schools and children is Volunteer mentors for young people, a one-to-one support scheme for marginalised young people. This project matches full-time mentors and part-time community mentors with young people at risk of offending and young offenders. Some examples are Bedfordshire Mentors and Peers Project (BedMAP) and On-Line Swansea. There are similar projects operating in Doncaster and East London. The On-Line helps up to 300 young people every year.
