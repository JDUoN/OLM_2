Landelijk Buro Vluchtelingenwerk from the Netherlands

 

The Story of Vluchtelingenwerk

Landelijk Buro Vluchtelingenwerk, translated as National Refugee Council Association, is a Dutch organisation run by volunteers to provide support, education and aids for refugees and asylum seekers in the Netherlands.

It is an independent organisation, which is represented in almost all municipalities and centres for asylum seekers. There are some 400 local working groups, ranging in size from a small foundation with only a handful of volunteers to large organisations with more than 30 paid staff and scores of volunteers. In 90 percent of all Dutch municipalities, a local Dutch Refugee Council foundation is working. Every day, more than 10,000 Dutch Refugee Council volunteers put their energy into working for people who have fled their countries and who hope to find a safe future in The Netherlands. The type of support the volunteers offer varies from personal encouragement during the asylum procedure to practical assistance when refugees attempt to build their own lives in Dutch society.

Recent years mark a profound change in the pattern and the nature of voluntary activities, which can be seen in an organisation like Vluchtelingenwerk which requires a large number of volunteers. One of the man challenges facing Vluchtelingenwerk is to recruit volunteers and to retain them. Some volunteers are affiliated with a certain refugee family and they need to stay as long as the family needs them. The drop in number of volunteers and their availability is a main concern for the effective running of the organisation.

There is also a shift in the profile of the volunteers, and the attitudes towards volunteering. The traditional volunteer was someone who was faithful to the organisation and would stay for many years. More recently, the volunteers is a trainee student who would stay with the organisation for a year at the most. There is also a shift from housewives to pre-pensioners and pensioners.

These changes could be attributed to the changes in the labour market and demographic patterns. More and more housewives are taking jobs, often part-time. Students, traditionally another source of volunteer force for a Vluchtelingenwerk, are having to take up jobs in order to support their living due to the reduction in study grants. Most of them do not have much time to be volunteers, although their social commitments are still maintained, as they show in their trainee periods.

(Written by Joël van Raak & Ruud van der Veen)

 

Reflection

  • How do changes in the work patterns and socio-cultural aspects of society affect volunteering as a form of active citizenship?
