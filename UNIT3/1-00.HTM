Overview

What forms of Active Citizenship are Recognised/Not Recognised

As we have seen in Themes 1 and 2, active citizenship implies some form of activity, individual and/or collective act of social responsibility, towards others or on behalf of others. It is also associated with some form of commitment - such as social, political, developmental - which could occur in one or more areas of life (e.g., family, work, voluntary activities, etc.). Active citizenship also involved participation and involvement in decision making processes that affect wider society. These processes of participation and involvement can be referred to as 'governance.'

Where active citizenship is carried out affects perceptions of which activities are recognised or legitimised as active citizenship. The general ideas of active citizenship, as something that is done on behalf of collectives implies that anyone can become an active citizen, and all kinds of positive activities towards others are considered as acts of active citizenship.

Is it really? Is that how the things work out in the real world? Are all active citizenship activities recognised equally? Are there some activities more equal than others? If so, why? These are the issues that we are going to explore in this theme.

We will explore these issues through stories from six European countries, a collection of stories of individuals, communities, and organisations, as follows:

  • Marlene in Britain (story of a woman whose active citizenship was not recognised until she came to the public domain)
  • Mojca in Slovenia (story of a woman whose life course enabled her to move to the public domain)
  • Jukka in Finland (story of a disabled man who was excluded since childhood, and later fought back to claim his place in the society)
  • Gypsy communities in Spain (story of a community whose activities are hardly recognised)
  • De Wrikker in Belgium (story of an organisation that enables its employees to express their active citizenship)
  • Landelijk Buro Vluchtelingenwerk in the Netherlands (story of an organisation that shows the changing face of active citizenship in contemporary society)

Before reading these stories, let's pause for a moment ...

Reflection

  • Think of individuals and/or organisations you are dealing with. Can you identify instances where their activities and contributions are hidden, ignored, or not recognised?
  • Are there cases where particular individuals' or organisations' contributions are more prominent?
  • What makes some activities and contributions more prominent and recognised than others? What are the factors that contribute to these differences?
