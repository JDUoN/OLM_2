Gypsy communities in Spain

 



Story of Gypsies

The Gypsy Communities in Spain provides an example of a community whose organisation and political participation is different from that of mainstream society.

Within a gypsy community, the decision making process is not one of a centralised process, nor are there Gypsy institutions that are involved in public affairs. In this sense, the Gypsy Community resembles a model of political organization whereby decisions are made based on loosely organised local groups.

Gypsies are a community with their own organisational structure, and traditional ways of life. Their presence and representation in traditional forms of politics is hidden.

Following are some examples of initiatives that are calling to change this pattern, and that are geared to bring their participation in the political processes to the forefront of society.

Drom Kotar Mestipen is an association of Gypsy Women whose main objective is to achieve Gypsy women's active involvement. They consider that Gypsy women are the victims of a two-fold exclusion, as Gypsies, and as women. Historically, women have been excluded from the activities in the public domain; their participation in the work domain or other public spheres is limited. The Work domain provides opportunities for people to take part in public-related activities, and to learn new skills. Excluded from access to these public spheres, gypsy women's participation has been limited to the family and the private domain. Gypsy women talk about the many activities that they carry out in their families:

We [...] help each other and it coincides with the fact that we are alone [stay at home] … . We lend each other money, we help each other to raise the children. If one was sick we help each other.

But these roles are not recognised according to maintream standards. Not only women, Gypsy men too, participate actively but are not recognized for it. People who do not participate in the established ways are suffering social exclusion.

Aleluya is another social movement with religious roots established to fight against social exclusion of gypsy communities.

The Gypsy university student movement is an organisation aimed at increasing the opportunities for Gypsies to have the opportunity to study at university.

The Centre for Gypsy Studies (CEG) at the University of Barcelona (see below for more information) is a research centre carrying out studies on issues related to the social exclusion of Gypsy communities. They also take part in applied and participatory projects (e.g., Ciudad Gitana (Gypsy City)) a project, to improve the participation of Gypsy communities based on their own needs and interests.

The Centre for Gypsy Studies (CEG) within the The Centre of Social and Educational Research at the University of Barcelona is carrying out research on the social exclusion issues that the Gypsy people face. A main theme of their research is to address the issues facing the Gypsy communities and to explore how to overcome the social exclusion. Following are two main reports stemming from the Centre's work that provides some recommendations on increasing the participation of Gypsy individuals in society:

  1. "Foment participation of Gypsy women and participation in the different spheres in the community" (Millán, C.; Sánchez, M.; Vargas, J.; 2000: 161);
  2. "Create a counsel of Gypsies, as an organism that makes decisions on the different actions that take place through CEG" (Millán, C.; Sánchez, M.; Vargas, J.; 2000: 162).
