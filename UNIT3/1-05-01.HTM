Work can be a source of active citizenship, or a manifestation of it. For some people, their work represents a challenge in itself, for others work is a way to meet a challenge. It can be a way of developing connections, of acquiring capacities and advancing a feeling of agency and self-determination. Some people learn skills at work which can be transferred to other areas of life. Of course, active citizenship is more than paid labour. Though for some people paid labour is their way of being active citizens, others want to do meaningful and useful things after, in addition to, to or instead of paid work. But some of these social and voluntary activities are less well acknowledged in society. De Wrikker is a good example of how work can be closely interwoven with active citizenship.

Some organisations like De Wrikker try to create a new kind of economy: the economy of solidarity. This is an economy that provides opportunities for all, including people who are excluded from the mainstream labour market. The model of 'working together' is an alternative to the principle of competition. This model not only relates to what happens inside an organisation, but also to its relations with society at large. It respects the interests of all 'stakeholders': employees, customers, and local community.

Carmen Mathijssen
