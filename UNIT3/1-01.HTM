Marlene from England

 



Marlene's Story

Marlene was born during the wartime and was an evacuee, which had a long lasting impact on her identity. The other influence on her identity was the Catholic religion. She left school at the age of 15 without qualifications - which also had an impact on her self-identity. Marlene joined the armed forces and worked abroad for four years where she met a man whom she married upon completion of her national service. She gave birth to five children, though within a few years her husband became violent towards her. She endured the unhappy marriage for the sake of the children. When the children grew up, she left the marital home and because a counsellor/advisor in a women's center.

As a child she was led to believe by the Catholic Church that becoming a mother was the most important thing for a woman. Her role as a mother, bringing up future citizens however, is not publicly recognised as being an active citizen. During this phase of her life Marlene obtained some educational qualifications and took a counselling course to help people with drug and alcohol problems. After this she left the marital home and did casual work in various shops and department stores. Although she had spent a large portion of her life caring for others and attending further education, the 'citizen' status of these activities contrasts sharply with those of some of the others, such as Mandy's.

In Marlene's case she was not being driven to speak out on behalf of others, her education was not overtly being used to help others - but the experiences were gradually building her identity so that she eventually had a sufficient sense of self to enable her to look for an environment where people had a collective mission to help others. She joined a women's centre. At first the experience was primarily a further support to her growing personal identity. She said:

It felt warm, it felt friendly, it felt like it was my place, it was a place I needed to be, I wanted to be there.

But it also gave her a step into a more public and collective world:

I think that was part of my sudden feeling of women, it was so exciting to think that women were gathering together.

From here, Marlene began to make connections with her own experiences and knowledge and she began to make use of those experiences to reach out to others:

I have children, I have two grand children I have had a hysterectomy, I have gone through domestic violence, I'm now separated from the man I married.

In addition she started to read 'a lot of books around the women's movement'. She became a counsellor in the centre, helping other abused women to find themselves. She also took time off to work in America in a centre for homeless women.

(Written by Julia Preece)
