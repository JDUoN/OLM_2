Salif's story

Salif was born in Northern Morocco after the second World War. Morocco was under 'protectorate' of Spain and France at that time. He grew up under the Spanish regime. After the independence of Morocco, they were confronted with a very strict and repressive Moroccan regime and the departure of a lot of economic and social capital. People of his generation felt they had to improve the situation of Morocco, his people. This explains why so many of his generation are very committed to the Moroccan cause. After independence he became very active in youth movements, youth centres, etc. These movements and centres were linked to political parties and aimed at teaching and education the youth (raising awareness) about political, social and syndical issues. From his secondary school onward, he was involved in a student movement, striving for democracy, freedom and against the repression of the royal regime. He enacted his commitment to this issue via the labour union, he didn't feel very politically connected. He also believed in changing and improving the country through the education of children, so he decided to become a teacher. He had a lot of frictions with the regime, refused his army service and found it safer to emigrate to Europe. He came to Belgium where he married a Belgian woman.

In Belgium he dedicated a lot of his time to the Moroccan cause, to the revolution against the repressive regime, still thinking he would return. When the Belgian government installed a migration stop in '74 it became clear that it was useless to think he would return and to invest in Morocco. He then involved himself in an Arabic department of the Catholic labour union. When he left the ACV, because of disagreements, he left the migration theme and got involved into international development cooperation and tried to find money for Moroccan projects.
