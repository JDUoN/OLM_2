Characteristics of active citizenship - different perspectives

First, let's reflect on the stories that you have read so far. You may have read all of them (24), or chosen to read a few that interested you. While you were reading these stories, you may have jotted down some notes, or given some thoughts to the guiding questions that appeared in each page.

Reflection

Take about 2 minutes to look at your notes, and reflect:

What kind of words did you use in order to describe the active citizenship of each individual? What kinds of contexts or places are they active in? Why are they active?

Compare your notes and thoughts with the following which is our own interpretation.

The active citizens used words such as a commitment to 'making a difference', 'getting things done' or 'changing things', wanting to 'help', 'doing something worthwhile', 'putting something back into the community', having a 'political consciousness', 'getting involved', 'tackling injustice' and 'improving conditions'. Can we use these words as indicators or characteristics of active citizenship?

Their active citizenship activities were taking place in a number of contexts such as at work, through the family environment (private domain), community (civil society) or through public, elected office (state domain).

Active citizenship was connected to personal needs such as 'personal development' 'doing more with one's life' and an 'opportunity to learn', as well as due to a sense of social responsibility.

We believe that active citizenship is some individual and/or collective act of social responsibility towards others or on behalf of others. What do you think?

Active citizenship is therefore understood as something one does for oneself as well as for others. Active citizenship is generally associated with 'doing': doing with others and for others. It is also associated with some form of cause - either social, political or developmental. These could occur in a variety of areas of life: in the family, at work, in the civil society, etc.

Active citizenship also involves participation and involvement in decision making processes that affect wider society. These processes of participation and involvement can be referred to as 'governance.'

Based on your notes, and our analysis, we'll try to develop a framework that identifies and recognises diversity of active citizenship.
