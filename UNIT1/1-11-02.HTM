Active citizens' descriptive stories - continued

Rok from Slovenia

Rok is also an example of an active citizens whose activities have been influenced by the political and economic developments in his country. While you read the story, make notes of the various social responsibilities he took up, and how these were influenced by the societal context.



Rok's story

Rok completed his high school in a rather liberal environment in a small town near the border with Italy, where he felt very much 'at home'. Then he moved to Ljubljana (the Capital of Slovenia) where he studied electrical engineering and later on got employment.

Rok is a successful businessman. After studying at university, he took a job as an engineer in a large state-run factory producing electrical appliances. Although quickly promoted, he was not completely satisfied with his career development, especially when he returned from military service. After a year's absence, his projects had not moved forward. This was the beginning of the economic restructuring, when many firms were uncertain about their future, cut back on investment, and dismissing skilled professionals.

However, private initiatives also started to develop in Slovenia at this time, mostly in the area of computer technology. Many educated young people wished to become involved in this sector. Rok found work as a manager in a commercial publishing firm. Because of the changing national economic situation, he and his business partner were able two years later to establish a private computer firm. This transition to the private business sector was an important turning point, not only in his career but in his private life.

Rok's firm quite rapidly became a successful enterprise. The success of the firm led to his involvement with issues arising from the transition process in Slovenia. Groups of people, marginalised and needing support, were seeking financial aid. Rok realised that people were living in a very uneven society where a large section of society is poor. The fragmentation of the socialist system has not been beneficial to every one. Rok could not help everyone financially. And he was left with the dilemma of whom to support. He thought just donating money is not an effective way to bring about equality.

Rok decided he should tackle only the issues he knew best. So he focussed his energy on his professional field - the development of information technology. Around 1995 he started to establish and gain proper status for the information and computing area within the national Chamber of Commerce. He was the first chairperson of the newly established association; now he is a member of its board.

Rok's critical awareness of the societal problems was long standing. As a young student he was involved in organising discussions on topics connected to the life of young people such as drug abuse, although at that time (the late 1970s) drug addiction was not yet a major issue in Slovenia. In addition, he regularly contributed to the college newspaper. Also while studying at the university he represented students in the faculty council.

In the mid-1990s, Rok joined the Rotary Club, where he still remains active. First he was the secretary and now he is a member of the steering committee. In both capacities he has been able to influence the club's activities and work.

On the other hand, he became very interested in the emergence of the new economy and the information society. He has devoted more and more of his time to it. 'I realised that the environment where I have been living does not comprehend it in the way it was introduced abroad, so I have increased my activities in this field. I started to write and to participate in round tables, seminars etc.'

He joined the Slovenian 'Informatics' Society, is now its vice-chairman, and has participated in various related projects. He has been especially active in a project on 'Slovenia as an information society', designed to promote the information society and lobby for increased awareness of this important issue. A particular concern is to influence key decision-making spheres (state administration, politics, economy) to encourage them to make good decisions. He helped prepare the blue book Slovenia as the Information Society. The government later adopted this book as its national strategic document in this field. Now Rok promotes the strategy by giving lectures at professional events organised by 'Informatics' and by the Research Association. In addition, he has a weekly column in one of the main national daily newspapers, where he focuses on the implications of information technology for people's lives. He also helps in establishing information networks at the national level as well as in joining international networks.

Rok's activities in the work and civil society domains intertwine with his private activities. His wife stopped working when he established his firm, but she is not excluded from his work. When he started the business there was no clear line between work and family life: 'The work was almost like a hobby.' Many new and interesting experiences were involved. He and his partner were relatively inexperienced, so they often discussed issues with their families and friends. When uncertain about how to manage charity activities, Rok involved his wife in the search for adequate solutions.
