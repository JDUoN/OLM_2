Active citizens' descriptive stories - continued

Alkuvoima in Finland

This is a story about an organisation, rather than about an individual. The word 'Alkuvoima' literally means Primal Force. Alkuvoima tells us a story of an organisation that works in the border area between 'civil society' and 'work'.



Alkuvoima's story

Alkuvoima has been established by a group of young farmers such as Anja (who tells this story) in order to fight the injustice they faced in their work. The young farmers in her community felt that they were treated unjustly by the established structures such as The Central Union of Agricultural Producers and Forest Owners (in short, MTK) and by the Finnish media. They took proactive action, and formed an activity group to improve the image of farmers and other country people. Anja explains more:

We then, I and my friends, when this kind of circle of friends was born in this activity, we established Alkuvoima (Primal force) activity group. It started because we young (farmers) never succeeded in achieving positions of influence (in MTK). Our way to act was too visible, critical and radical. So, they did not at once have courage to trust us. For this reason, we decided, that it is all right. We can act by ourselves too. Finland was then going to join the European Union - a very pointed antagonism between countryside and city. There were young women in the same situation. They had also experienced the loneliness of mother of being a young children, and they had also experienced the lack of 'soul mates' in the neighbourhood. Then men started to join our group too. We created it as an informal group, and it became very quickly a kind of study circle, we supported each other, and it became also a demonstration group capable of reacting very fast.

Alkuvoima was born in 1993 among young and young-at heart farmers in South-East-Finland. The aim of the group is to maintain the Finnish countryside as a viable and enjoyable environment. Parallelly, the aim is to improve the image and position of 'farmer sisters and brothers'. Alkuvoima has about 40 members, mostly young farmers, but not only: the professions vary from a baker to a diplomat, a school headmaster to a university scientist. There is no age limit and both women and men are welcome. However, there is a nucleus of seven women who have run most of the activities of the group.

Alkuvoima has no formal organisational hierarchy, but the naturally grown nucleus of seven women has run most of the activities. According to Anja, one of the wonderful features of the group is that the women feel that they are whole-heartedly encouraged in their activities by the men.

Another important feature of the group has been that instead of writing meeting protocols and using energy in other less productive activities, the group has worked to change the world by letting people know how young farmers saw the situation in the country. The group has written press notices, arranged street campaigns and manifestations, met political decision-makers and contacted different organisations. Anja also describes how the study circle oriented itself to Europe.

The group has also studied how the European Union is functioning. In this learning process networking has played an important role:

We established two study circles in -94. The first one was Euroopan kasvot and the second one was Face of Europe (The names are equivalent, one in Finnish, the other in English.). Euroopan kasvot study circle started to study how the EU is functioning and what are citizen's possibilities to influence Europe. This Face of Europe began to study English, agricultural English, and made contacts with farmers in the EU. We did not want to believe in propaganda only… .

 

I myself had visited Austria as an exchange student and have entered into correspondence with foreign people since 11 years old. I suggested that: 'hi, this is one of our principles, this low budget activity, so, let's send friendly letters to Middle-European agricultural journals. We can ask them to publish for free our pen-friend advertisements: we want to correspond with colleagues. And perhaps they can send us some material'. The consequence was that we received over a hundred answers. We sent our advertisements to England, Scotland, Wales, Ireland, Belgium, France, Germany and Austria, to eight journals. It was not part of the policy of every journal to publish advertisements of this kind. in Belgium, in England, in Scotland and in Germany they published. And from Austria they sent us journals, and from Germany they sent a half of an annual volume. And we met these pen-friends.
