Patricia's story

Patricia grew up in a working class family, next to the church tower, after the first World War. The stories and experiences her father and relatives shared with her about the War had a deep influence on her and on the values she made her own. As a child, she got to sit with her uncle in his barber shop and she listened to all the conversations and debates about politics, societal problems, possible solutions that took place. What she learned in the barbershop is sometimes contradictory to what she was told at school. This experience really shaped her value pattern and her way of thinking (critical). Within her family and neighbourhood she witnessed several injustices towards the working class people which induced her devotion to the improvement of the working men's and women's lives. She participated in the Catholic movement; from the youth movement to the working class women's movement. She learned about and took part actively in the emancipation of working class and more specifically of the working class women. She became a social worker and the first years of her career were devoted to improving the situation (also structurally) of the families of railway workers. Through her work as a social worker and her participation in the women's movement, she got involved in local politics (own choice + asked by the party), in the Christian Democratic Party (CVP). From that time onwards, she started to devote her life to politics (local and later national) and was quite successful (the road is not without bends), especially at a time where women were a rare species in national politics.
