Pigga's story

Pigga is a former teacher. She is a Lappish Same, currently a reindeer farm housewife, active in matters related to ethnic and cultural Same issues and reindeer economy as well as in artistic and tourism-related tasks.

She has lived most of her life in Finland's most Northern communities. In her childhood and and youth the Same life was still traditional, although not in any way primitive. Reindeer were important both economically and for home life and cultural traditions. She compares the present way of life to the earlier by saying that actually everything has stayed pretty much the same. Although modern technology has made it even to Same farms, the family and basic structure of cultural practices have according to her experience not changed. Children in the family become now real Same in essentially the same way as when she grew up.

Her activism both professionally and in relation to her native origin was based positively in her home life, where the culture was acquired in a natural and unconstrained way. This contrasted strongly with boarding school experiences in Sodankylä. Because of the long distances children lived most of the terms in a boarding school under close surveillance. The teachers were at that time not Same, and they had no commitment to the native culture. On the contrary, they wanted to integrate the children to Finnish society as fully as possible. The discipline was strict, and they were e.g. forbidden to speak their own language.

After school Pigga gained various experiences of life. Understanding how important one's own culture is, she acted as a teacher. She lived in other places, too, so she knows Finnish ways of life from many points of view. Because of that she feels satisfied having married with Same reindeer farmer and returned to the core of Same community. The Northern distances, and necessity of developing livelihood are central to her, but she feels happy that the various tasks can be done with her own family and tribe. Even a modern Same farm is an enterprise, which must be developed constantly. Pigga does not know how many reindeer they have, but answers in the Same manner: "There are a few on the yard, and some more on the other side of the tree - and there may be enough of them even to the unseen side of the hill." The reindeer do not migrate nowadays the same way as before, although they still are kept relatively free. This has created a need to find more nourishment and Pigga has taken an active role in experiments related to this. She is also artistic, trying out both traditional handicraft methods and later forms of artistic expression. This is also related to tourism, which has become more and more important in Lapland during the years.

The Same have been relatively successful in improving their position in relation to the state, even their status as an original native ethnic group has been ratified and they have got their own parliament-like board. Pigga's family is involved in these developments. Adult education is common in municipalities of Lapland, and the adult education centres have also arranged open university courses. These opportunities activated Pigga to improve her own resources also by university studies, leading eventually to an EdM degree. Research methods are useful also in developing methods of reindeer farming. At the time when Pigga was telling this story, she was just going to a press conference on the results of these studies. She has also been presented on Finnish TV programmes, which often shows the colourful Same life.

Pigga invited us to come to the North and see by our own eyes what the life is like there.
